HFL_1, A,B,C - Fire Dynamics Modelling for understanding Wildfire Behaviour

Fire Dynamics modelling requires understanding of fire-driven fluid flow. Wildland fires are a growing cause of concern, with frequent wildfires spreading to urban communities, especially in the United States of America and Europe. These fires often propagate via ember transport causing nearby trees or materials to ignite. Ignition depends on a variety of factors, such as moisture content, heat transfer mode (convection/conduction/radiation), ignition source, and geometry to name a few. In this type of fires, the boundary conditions are continuously varying in both space and time, making it very difficult to model. To further complicate matters, for a given problem we rarely know precisely what the boundary conditions are—we must rely on estimates of mean winds, mean moisture content, and mean ambient temperatures. This project focuses on different aspects of developing a CFD model for ignition and surface fire spread.

There are 3 openings for this project.

For Option A the project will focus on benchmarking the application of numerical modeling and risk assessment of wildfires, analysing the pros and cons of the present tools and methods including FDS and OpenFOAM, with consideration of machine learning approaches.

For Option B the project will focus on quantifying mesh indipendency in Fire Dynamic Simulator (FDS) for three different classical fire scenarios, to optimize the accuracy vs cost of the numerical simulations.

For Option C the project will focus on quantifying the effect of moisture on fire spread in a classical wildfire scenario, with the aim of helping understand the ways climate change is creating potentially more fire hazardous regions in areas which were once too moist to ignite.

These projects combine both fundamental physical phenomena, engineering and computer science skills, and gives the student a unique insight into the field of fire science, a field linking together different disciplines including Engineering, Geology, Physics, Ecology, Computer Science, Geography and Policy.

HFL_2, A,B - Lithium-ion battery calendar life and cycle life analysis

Lithium-ion batteries have been employed as energy sources in a number of electric devices, owing primarily to their higher energy density and lower cost compared to alternative candidates. In recent years, the market volume for batteries in the transportation industry, namely for electric or hybrid cars, has grown quickly. Side reactions and degradation processes can result in a variety of unwanted consequences that cause capacity loss in lithium-ion batteries. Battery voltage, capacity output, ageing history, and temperature are all factors that can influence capacity loss and ageing. Calendar life and cycle life studies are included in the ageing analysis. A calendar life analysis involves aging the battery at open circuit at different constant state-of-charges (SOCs). For a cycle life analysis, the battery is aged using several constant C-rate charge-discharge cycling schemes.

There are 2 openings for this project.

For Option A the project will focus on using high-performance workstations here at KCL with appropriate multiphysics software for optimzation and battery modelling: The student will be given access to an experimental database for model development and validation. The student will optimize parameters of the lumped model against experimental data required for calendar life analysis

For Option B, the project will focus on using high-performance workstations here at KCL with appropriate multiphysics software for optimizing cycle life analysis with charge-discharge at different C-rates. Finally, the models capability to predict the cell state-of-health variation with time

HFL_3, A - Electric vehicle charge speed optimization for next-gen transport

Lithium-ion batteries have been employed as energy sources in a number of electric devices, owing primarily to their higher energy density and lower cost compared to alternative candidates. In recent years, the market volume for batteries in the transportation industry, namely for electric or hybrid cars, has grown quickly. As batteries grow in size and power density, there has been an increased need in providing charge to them at faster and faster rates, to enable them to be used in the transport system for electric vehicles. With fast charging, however challenges such as degradation have to be understood.

There is 1 opening for this project.

The student will be studying pulse charigng for electric vehicle charge speed with minimal degradation, using multiphysics modelling and validating with experimental data

HFL_4, A,B - Thermal-electrochemical battery full load cycle (charge-discharge) simulation models for different geometries

Lithium-ion batteries have been employed as energy sources in a number of electric devices, owing primarily to their higher energy density and lower cost compared to alternative candidates. In many applications, the thermal characteristics of lithium-ion batteries during charge and discharge are crucial. Battery thermal management, battery degradation, ageing modelling, and battery management system design are all crucial in aerospace and automotive applications.

For MEng students, this individual project can be linked to a future 4th year MEng group project.

There are 2 opening for this project.

Option A: The student will be studying a thermal-electrochemical coupled model that predicts core temperature rather than just surface temperature, unlike previous models in literature, to try to tackle an important need that is critical for battery safety, reliability, and lifespan. In this option, the student will focus on a high density cylindrical cell.

Option B: The student will be studying a thermal-electrochemical coupled model that predicts core temperature rather than just surface temperature, unlike previous models in literature, to try to tackle an important need that is critical for battery safety, reliability, and lifespan. In this option, the student will focus on a high density pouch cell.

HFL_5, A,B - Computational Fluid Dynamic modelling of geometries for Formula Electric vehicles

KCL Engineering is looking to possibly join formula electric in the coming years, and we are starting to develop undergraduate projects that are conducive to the development of the core needs for a formula student car. These 2 projects will use Computational Fluid dynamic simulation software Star-CCM to model flow and heat transfer phenomena for different vehicle builds.

There are 2 openings for this project. For MEng students, this individual project can be linked to a future 4th year MEng group project.

For Option A the project will focus on the flow around a battery pack with the aim to optimize battery pack geometry to reduce drag in vehicle while still maximizing power density.

For Option B the project will focus on the flow around a battery pack with the aim to optimize cooling of a battery pack during operation of the vehicle.
